轮入道 Wa nyūdō

Type:
Supernatural phenomenon - Fire wheel yokai

Features:
Rolling flaming wheel with a human head, associated with punishment and torment

Appearance:
Large, burning wheel with a fierce human face or head, often depicted screaming or glaring

Distribution:
Appears on streets, especially at night, commonly in Japanese folklore

Ethnicity:
Native Japanese yokai

Names:
Wa nyūdō (Japanese for "wheel monk"), Fire Wheel, Flaming Wheel Spirit

Dynasty:
Prominent in the Heian and Muromachi periods

Basic Introduction

Wa nyūdō is a fearsome yokai in Japanese folklore, depicted as a massive wooden wheel engulfed in flames with a furious human face or head at its center. The name, meaning "wheel monk," originates from the tale of a monk cursed for his sins, doomed to an eternity of fiery torment. This eerie yokai is said to appear on roads and streets, primarily at night, spreading fear and misfortune wherever it goes.

According to traditional legends, Wa nyūdō rolls through towns and villages, relentlessly seeking victims to punish or terrify. Its presence serves as a grim reminder of the consequences of wrongdoing and the inescapable nature of karmic retribution. As a guardian of the border between the living and the dead, Wa nyūdō embodies both punishment and spiritual justice, making it a powerful symbol in Japanese folklore.

Behavioral Characteristics

The Fear of the Night:

At night, if people hear the sound of a rolling wheel, they should immediately avoid it; otherwise, they may be caught and have their souls taken away.

Repelling Evil Spirits:

In some stories, people can avoid Wa nyūdō’s attacks by offering prayers or showing repentance, symbolizing his role as an enforcer of order.

Knowledgeable:

Wa nyūdō is believed to have the ability to perceive the hearts of humans, discerning their virtues and sins. He serves as a reminder for people to correct their behavior. In some legends, he is portrayed as revealing secrets of the underworld, but only at a great cost.

"Wa nyūdō of Kyoto’s Streets."

The legendary yokai was believed to roam Kyoto during the dead of night, bringing terror to those who saw his blazing form. He is often illustrated as a burning ox cart wheel with a monk’s anguished face in the center, a symbol of karmic retribution.
